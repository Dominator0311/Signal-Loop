# The "Triple Whammy" — NSAID + ACE-I/ARB + Diuretic = AKI

## Primary Evidence

**Lapi F, Azoulay L, Yin H, et al. Concurrent use of diuretics, angiotensin converting enzyme inhibitors, and angiotensin receptor blockers with non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs and risk of acute kidney injury: nested case-control study. BMJ. 2013;346:e8525.**

URL: https://www.bmj.com/content/346/bmj.e8525

### Key Findings:
- Current use of a double therapy (diuretic + ACE-I/ARB) combined with NSAIDs was associated with a **31% increased rate of acute kidney injury** compared to double therapy alone
- The risk was highest in the first 30 days of triple combination use
- Rate ratio: 1.31 (95% CI 1.12-1.53)

### Mechanism:
The three drug classes together attack renal perfusion from three directions:

1. **NSAID** — inhibits prostaglandin-mediated afferent arteriolar vasodilation. In a kidney dependent on prostaglandins to maintain perfusion (as in volume-depleted or elderly patients), this removes the last compensatory mechanism.

2. **ACE-I/ARB** — inhibits angiotensin II-mediated efferent arteriolar vasoconstriction. This reduces filtration pressure.

3. **Diuretic** — reduces intravascular volume, decreasing renal perfusion pressure.

When all three are active simultaneously, the kidney loses its ability to maintain glomerular filtration rate. The result is pre-renal AKI that can progress to intrinsic damage if not reversed.

### Clinical Context:
- The triple combination accounts for **10-15% of community-acquired AKI admissions** in elderly patients (Thomas MC, Nephrology 2000)
- Most cases are reversible if the NSAID is stopped promptly
- Risk is highest in: elderly, diabetics, those with pre-existing CKD, dehydrated patients
- Even short courses (7 days) can precipitate AKI in vulnerable patients

## Risk Stratification

| Patient Factor | Effect on Risk |
|---------------|---------------|
| Age >70 | Significantly increased (reduced renal reserve) |
| Existing CKD (eGFR <60) | Very high risk (limited compensatory capacity) |
| Diabetes mellitus | Increased (microvascular disease) |
| Heart failure | Very high (already volume-dependent) |
| Dehydration / acute illness | Dramatically increased |
| High-dose NSAID | Higher risk than low-dose |
| Long-duration NSAID | Cumulative risk |

## Application to Margaret:

Margaret has ALL major risk factors:
- Age 72 (>70)
- CKD stage 3b (eGFR 42)
- Type 2 diabetes (10 years)
- Currently on lisinopril (ACE-I) + furosemide (diuretic)
- Proposed: ibuprofen (NSAID) = completes the triple whammy

Adding ibuprofen to her regimen would be the textbook "triple whammy" scenario with compounding risk from her age, diabetes, and already-declining renal function. The probability of precipitating AKI is clinically unacceptable.

## Safe Alternatives When NSAIDs Are Contraindicated:

1. **Paracetamol** — no renal impact, effective for mild-moderate OA pain
2. **Topical NSAIDs** — minimal systemic absorption, does not contribute to triple-whammy
3. **Non-pharmacological** — physiotherapy, TENS, heat therapy, exercise
4. **Weak opioids** (short-term only) — codeine, tramadol (with appropriate caution in elderly)
